# Tacit to Text

**Use when:** You've made, prototyped, drawn, or experienced something in your design research and now need to translate it into academic prose. This is the Polanyi (1966) and Sennett (2008) move — naming what you already know without losing what made it knowable in the first place.

## System prompt

> I am a design researcher trying to put tacit, material, or embodied knowledge into academic prose. When I describe something I made, drew, or experienced, your job is to ask me clarifying questions that help me name what I already know. Do not paraphrase. Do not generate descriptions. Surface the assumptions I am making.

## How to use it in LM Studio

1. Open the **Chat** tab.
2. Clear any default system prompt and paste the prompt above.
3. Describe the artifact or experience in plain, informal language — the way you'd describe it to a friend. Don't try to sound academic. The whole point is to start from where the knowing actually lives.
4. Answer the model's questions in writing. Your answers, lightly edited, often become the first draft of a section.

## Example queries

> I built a paper prototype of an interface for a community archive. The whole thing fell apart when I tried to fold the third flap, and that's when I realized the navigation structure I had in mind wasn't going to work. I want to write about this but everything I try sounds like a project report.

> I keep saying my fieldwork was "embodied" because I walked the route every day for three weeks. I don't actually know what I mean by that. Help me figure it out.

> I drew a diagram of the train line with one continuous black bar to mark where it's elevated. When I showed it to a colleague she said it looked aggressive. I want to write about that — but how?

## What to expect

The model should ask things like *"You said the prototype 'fell apart' — physically, conceptually, or both?"* or *"What does 'aggressive' mean here — about the line, about the diagram, about your colleague's reading?"* If the model starts producing the academic prose *for* you, the prompt isn't holding — switch back to a stricter framing or try a more capable model.

This prompt is the most useful one for translating making-based research into writing-based research.
